tagline: A field guide for educators who are done choosing between theory and practice.
summary: >
  A collection of 17 essays on the architecture of real learning — from the
  neuroscience of the language freeze to why most courses are structurally
  broken before the first lesson is even taught. Written for practitioners, not
  theorists, it moves between classroom-level craft (teacher talk time,
  scaffolding, presence) and system-level design (curriculum alignment,
  outcomes, online pedagogy), grounded throughout in research but never trapped
  by it.
problem: >
  Most teacher training treats classroom craft and curriculum design as separate
  disciplines — you get pedagogy tips in one workshop and syllabus templates in
  another, and nobody shows you how they're supposed to work together. Teachers
  end up either over-managing their own voice (killing good TTT because someone
  told them silence = skill) or delivering beautifully designed curricula that
  ignore the affective, relational reality of the room. This book treats
  teaching and designing as one job, not two.
takeaways:
  - How to audit their own talk time for impact, not just quantity
  - The difference between accommodation and differentiation — and why
    conflating them wastes both
  - How to design curriculum with structural alignment so outcomes, activities,
    and assessment actually match
  - What's really happening neurologically when a student "freezes," and how to
    design around it, not punish it
  - Why teacher wellbeing is a design variable, not a personal failing
  - How to build classroom culture that survives without relying on personal
    charisma
  - Practical scaffolding techniques that hold up across age groups
  - What's structurally different about teaching adult learners vs. young
    learners
  - How to close the "age gap" in online teaching contexts
