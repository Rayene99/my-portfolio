People often use *"curriculum design"* and *"instructional design"* interchangeably; but **they're not the same thing.** As someone who works at the intersection of both, here's how I think about the distinction:

Curriculum design answers the big *"what."*
It defines the scope, sequence, and goals of a learning program. What will learners know and be able to do by the end? In what order should concepts be introduced? What standards or competencies does this course address? Curriculum design is *strategic*, it maps the **destination.**

Instructional design answers the *"how."*
It focuses on how that content is actually delivered, the activities, materials, assessments, and learning experiences that help a specific learner reach specific objectives. Instructional design is *tactical*, it builds the **road**.

Think of it this way: A curriculum designer might decide that students need to master academic writing across a semester. The instructional designer then figures out how to scaffold that skill week by week, which mentor texts to use, what feedback cycles to build in, and how to check for understanding along the way.

Where it gets interesting: In many educational settings, the same person does both, often without realising they're wearing two different hats. Recognising the difference helps you ask better questions at each stage and avoid the trap of jumping to "how" before you've fully answered "what."

Both fields share a common goal: meaningful, purposeful learning. But they operate at different altitudes, and good education needs both.
