If you’ve spent five minutes in a teacher training program, you’ve heard the golden rule: Minimize Teacher Talking Time (TTT)

We’ve been conditioned to view our own voices as the enemy of progress, a barrier between the student and the learning. We track our TTT percentages like we’re monitoring a leak in a ship. 

But here’s the unpopular opinion: TTT isn't a "waste of time." It’s a precision tool.


When we push "minimal TTT" too far, we risk leaving our students in a vacuum of silence where they lack the very input they need to grow. It’s time we shift the conversation from Quantity to Quality.

Why your voice actually matters:

1️⃣ The "Comprehensible Input" Factor: For language learners or students tackling complex concepts, your voice is the primary blueprint. They need to hear a proficient speaker model nuance, tone, and logic.

2️⃣ The Power of Storytelling: A well-timed, 2-minute anecdote can anchor a dry concept in a student’s memory far better than a 10-minute "student-led" discovery task that leaves them confused.

3️⃣ Scaffolding through "Thinking Aloud": When you verbalize your thought process, you aren't just "talking", you are teaching them \*how\* to think. 

4️⃣ The Human Connection: Education is social. Sometimes, students just need to hear their mentor be a human. That rapport is the "secret sauce" that drives motivation.

↗️ From "Minimal" to "Optimal"

The goal shouldn't be to shut up; it should be to SPEAK WITH INTENT> Bad TTT:

Giving 15 minutes of clunky, repetitive instructions that could have been a handout. 

✅ Good TTT: A 5-minute explanation that sparks a "lightbulb moment" and sets the stage for 30 minutes of deep, independent work.

📌 Let’s stop counting the minutes and start counting the IMPACT. If your talking is providing the spark, the model, or the clarity they need, don't be afraid to take the mic. 🎤
