What You Don't Say Matters Just as Much as What You Do!

I spent years perfecting my lesson plans, hunting for the right text, crafting clever discussion questions. I thought good teaching was about the content. Then I noticed something: two teachers could use identical materials, but one classroom felt energized and safe while the other felt flat and anxious.

**The difference? Paralinguistic communication.**

Your pace. Your silence. The tone shift when a student takes an intellectual risk. The way you look up from your notes. Whether you rush through difficult moments or linger with them. Most English teacher training focuses on what to teach. Almost none addresses how to use your presence to teach.

Here's what changed for me:

* A strategic pause after asking a question signals that you genuinely want to hear their thinking, not that you're waiting to fill silence.
* Slowing down when discussing vulnerable topics creates psychological safety. Speed signals urgency; pace signals presence.
* Tone variation prevents the monotone that signals indifference. Your excitement about a topic is contagious. Your skepticism shuts students down.
* Deliberate movement shows which parts of the room matter. Standing near students sharing work says "I'm listening." Pacing during an explanation suggests confidence.
* Silence after a student speaks gives their words weight. Jumping in immediately suggests their contribution wasn't worth processing.

The irony? These tools ***cost nothing.*** They require no new curriculum or technology. But they transform how students experience your classroom.
