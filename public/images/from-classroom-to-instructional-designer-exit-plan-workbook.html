Part 1 of 5

Map the Field

"Instructional design" isn't one job. It's a whole neighborhood of jobs — and you need to know which house you're knocking on before you can dress for the interview.

Here's the short version: teaching is about delivering learning live, one room at a time, usually to people who didn't choose to be there. Instructional design is about architecting learning — building the course, module, or resource once, so it can teach well without you standing next to it. Same underlying skill (helping people learn something they didn't know before). Different delivery mechanism, different audience, and — this part matters — usually a very different paycheck and schedule.

You don't need to pick a lane forever. You do need to pick one to aim at first, because it changes what your portfolio, resume, and applications should emphasize.

Where the field is heading AI hasn't replaced instructional designers — but it has raised the bar. Employers increasingly expect comfort with AI-assisted workflows (drafting scripts, generating quiz variations, building branching scenarios faster), and portfolios that show you can direct those tools well tend to stand out. The core skill hiring managers still care about most is the same one you already have: knowing what actually helps someone learn.

Translate Your Experience

You are already doing roughly 80% of this job. Nobody ever handed you the dictionary for it. Here it is.

Hiring managers outside education don't know what "differentiated instruction" means, and they will not Google it. They also don't know that it's a harder, more sophisticated skill than most corporate "audience segmentation" work. Your job in this section isn't to learn new skills — it's to relabel the ones you have so a stranger reading your resume for eight seconds understands their value immediately.

Two frameworks worth knowing before your first interview

Framework

ADDIE

Analyze the problem and audience → Design the objectives and structure → Develop the actual materials → Implement with real learners → Evaluate what worked. It's the classic, linear ID process — and if you've ever planned, taught, and reflected on a unit, you've already run this cycle.

Framework

SAM

The Successive Approximation Model — a faster, more iterative alternative to ADDIE. Build a rough draft fast, get feedback, revise, repeat. Many corporate teams favor it because it ships usable training sooner. Mentioning both in an interview signals you've done your homework.

Part 3 of 5

Build Your Portfolio

In this field, your portfolio doesn't support your resume. It is your resume. Hiring managers want to see how you think, not read a list of adjectives about it.

Here's the good news: you don't need "real" ID experience to build one. You need three to five honest, well-documented projects that show your process — not fifteen rushed ones that show your desperation. Quality and range beat quantity every time.

A strong portfolio project shows four things

  • The problem — who needed to learn what, and why it mattered
  • Your process — how you analyzed it and made design decisions (this is what separates a designer from someone who just made a pretty slide deck)
  • The solution — what you actually built
  • The (intended) impact — how you'd measure whether it worked, even if this one's hypothetical for now

Six projects you can start this month with what you already have

Project idea

Redesign a favorite lesson as an eLearning storyboard

Take a unit you know cold and rebuild it as a self-paced module: screens, narration script, and interactions.

Project idea

Turn a worksheet into an interactive mini-module

Rebuild a favorite activity in Canva, H5P, or Articulate Rise so it's clickable, not printable.

Project idea

Formalize a new-teacher onboarding module

If you've ever informally trained a new colleague, you already have the raw material for a real onboarding project.

Project idea

Convert a curriculum unit into a full ID document

Needs analysis, learning objectives, assessment plan, and a sample storyboard — this is the piece that proves you understand the whole process, not just the pretty parts.

Project idea

Script a microlearning video from a 5-minute reteach

Take a concept you've re-explained a dozen times and turn it into a tight 3–5 minute video script and storyboard.

Project idea

Design one AI-enabled learning experience

A simple branching scenario or a custom AI tutor persona shows employers you're comfortable directing today's tools, not just aware of them.

Part 4 of 5

Write a Resume That Stands Out

A teaching resume says "responsible for." An ID resume says "designed," "built," "reduced," "increased." Same person. Different dialect.

Structure it like this

  1. Headline / summary — two to three lines pitching your transferable value. Not "former teacher seeking a new challenge."
  2. Core skills — a keyword-rich line: instructional design methods (ADDIE, SAM), tools, and one or two soft skills that actually matter (stakeholder communication, needs analysis).
  3. Portfolio link — near the top, not buried at the bottom. For a career changer, this is doing more work than any bullet point.
  4. Experience — translated using Part 2's dictionary, with metrics wherever you honestly have them.
  5. Education & certifications — your degree still counts. List it plainly; don't apologize for it not saying "instructional design."

General

The one that actually works best

Referrals & direct outreach

Roles are often filled before they're widely posted. A warm introduction skips the queue entirely — more on this in the Bonus section.

Common interview questions — and how to think about them

They askYour angle as a former teacher
"Walk me through your design process."Structure your answer around ADDIE or SAM, even if you never called it that while you were doing it.
"Tell me about designing for a difficult or resistant audience."Classroom management stories translate directly — reframe the audience as "learners with low initial buy-in."
"How do you handle feedback from stakeholders or subject matter experts?"Parent communication, admin feedback, and peer coaching are all stakeholder management. Say so.
"Walk us through a project in your portfolio."Rehearse the Challenge → Process → Solution → Impact structure for each piece until it's second nature.
"How do you measure whether training worked?"Connect directly to your formative/summative assessment experience.
"What tools have you used?"Be honest. Emphasize that you're a fast, self-directed learner — and point to a portfolio piece as proof.
"Why are you leaving teaching?"Frame it as moving toward something, not fleeing something. Talk about what draws you to ID, not what exhausted you about the classroom.
The design challenge Many ID interviews include a take-home exercise — "storyboard a 10-minute module on X." Use ADDIE as visible scaffolding in your response so your thinking is easy to follow. Don't overbuild for a timed exercise; a clear, well-reasoned partial solution beats a rushed, polished one. If you're allowed to ask clarifying questions first, ask them — it's exactly the instinct a real ID needs.

Bonus — Mindset

The Fears Nobody Puts in the Job Guide

"I don't have an ID degree."

Most postings list "or equivalent experience" for a reason — employers increasingly weigh a strong portfolio and tool proficiency over a specific degree title. Your master's still counts. Your portfolio is the "equivalent experience."

"I'm too experienced in teaching to start over."

You're not starting over. You're transferring. Nobody transferring a decade of expertise into a new label is starting at zero — Part 2 of this workbook is proof.

"I can't afford to just quit."

You don't have to. Most successful transitions happen while still employed — building the portfolio on evenings and weekends, applying once it's ready. There's no prize for leaping without a landing spot.

"What if I'm not creative enough?"

You planned 180 days of instruction a year on a budget of index cards and hope. You are creative enough. This field rewards clear thinking more than it rewards flair.

Bonus — Glossary

The ID Glossary

Twenty terms that'll come up in interviews and job postings, so you're never quietly Googling under the table.

ADDIE

Analyze, Design, Develop, Implement, Evaluate — the classic ID process model.

SAM

Successive Approximation Model — a faster, iterative alternative to ADDIE.

LMS

Learning Management System — the platform that hosts and tracks training (e.g., Google Classroom, Moodle, Cornerstone).

LXP

Learning Experience Platform — a more learner-driven, Netflix-style cousin of the LMS.

SCORM

A technical standard that lets eLearning content "talk" to an LMS and report completion/scores.

xAPI (Tin Can)

A newer standard for tracking learning experiences beyond just an LMS — including real-world and informal learning.

UDL

Universal Design for Learning — designing accessibly from the start, not retrofitting later.

Bloom's Taxonomy

A hierarchy of cognitive skill levels (remember → understand → apply → analyze → evaluate → create), used to write learning objectives.

ILT / VILT

Instructor-Led Training / Virtual ILT — live facilitation, in person or online.

Microlearning

Short, focused learning content — usually under five minutes, built around one objective.

Rapid authoring

Building eLearning quickly using tools like Rise or Storyline, rather than custom code.

Storyboard

A screen-by-screen plan of a module's content, visuals, and interactions before it's built.

Needs analysis

Figuring out whether training is actually the right solution, and what it needs to cover if so.

Gap analysis

Comparing current performance to desired performance to define what training must close.

KSA

Knowledge, Skills, and Abilities — the building blocks a learning objective is meant to build.

Stakeholder

Anyone with a say in the training — your manager, an SME, a client. Think: admin, parent, or department head.

SME

Subject Matter Expert — the person who knows the content; your job is to know how people learn it.

Learning objective

A specific, measurable statement of what a learner will be able to do afterward.

Kirkpatrick Model

Four levels of evaluating training: Reaction, Learning, Behavior, Results.

Job aid

A quick-reference tool (checklist, one-pager) that supports performance in the moment, instead of a full course.
