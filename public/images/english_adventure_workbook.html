📖 Reading Passage — "The Girl Who Talked to Stars"

Every evening, nine-year-old Layla climbed to the rooftop of her house with a notebook and a flashlight. She believed, with her whole heart, that the stars were listening.

"Tonight was hard," she wrote, the pencil scratching softly. "I got the spelling wrong in front of everyone and my face went completely red." She looked up. Orion's Belt sparkled back at her, steady and bright.

Layla didn't know the science of stars — not yet. But she knew that some things are too large and too old to be bothered by a spelling mistake. And that, somehow, was enough to make her breathe again.

The next morning, she walked into school with her notebook tucked under her arm, ready to try once more.

Listen & Learn 👂
Becoming a powerful, active listener
🎧 Active Listening Strategies
🎯 Focus
Remove distractions. Look at the speaker. Think only about what they're saying right now.
🧠 Predict
Guess what comes next. It keeps your brain engaged and improves your listening speed.
🔑 Key Words
Listen for important words — names, numbers, feelings. You don't need to catch everything.
❓ Clarify
It's okay to ask someone to repeat themselves. "Could you say that again, please?"
🖊️ Note-Take
Write key words while listening — not full sentences. Draw diagrams if it helps!
💬 Respond
Show you understood by nodding, summarising, or asking a related question.

Unit Seven
Think Big! 🧠
Developing your critical thinking and reasoning skills
💡Critical thinking means not accepting things at face value. Ask WHY. Ask HOW. Ask WHO BENEFITS. Ask WHAT IF?
🔍 The 5 Questions of Critical Thinking
1. WHAT?
What exactly is being said? Summarise it in your own words.
2. WHO?
Who wrote or said this? Do they have any bias or agenda?
3. WHY?
Why is this important? What problem does it address?
4. WHAT IF?
What if the opposite were true? What if things were different?
5. SO WHAT?
Why should I care? What does this change for me or the world?
6. EVIDENCE?
What proof is there? Is it from a reliable and trusted source?

Idioms & Expressions 🌈
Unlocking the colourful, figurative side of English
💡An idiom is an expression whose meaning is different from the literal words. Example: "It's raining cats and dogs" doesn't mean animals are falling — it means it's raining very heavily!
🎴 Idiom Gallery
🐛 "Under the weather"
Meaning: Feeling slightly ill or unwell
"I can't come to school — I'm feeling a bit under the weather."
🧱 "Hit the nail on the head"
Meaning: To be exactly right about something
"When she said the problem was lack of teamwork, she hit the nail on the head."
🥄 "Bite off more than you can chew"
Meaning: To take on more than you can handle
"I signed up for 5 clubs and bit off more than I could chew."
☁️ "Every cloud has a silver lining"
Meaning: There is something positive in every bad situation
"I failed, but learned so much — every cloud has a silver lining."
🎯 "The ball is in your court"
Meaning: It's your turn to take action or decide
"I've told her how I feel — the ball is in her court now."
🌊 "Bite the bullet"
Meaning: To endure a painful or difficult situation bravely
"He didn't want to apologise, but decided to bite the bullet."

😤 Managing Stage Fright — 6 Strategies
😤 1. Breathe
Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 4, exhale for 6. Repeat 3×. It signals calm to your brain.
🔁 2. Rehearse
Practise in front of a mirror, then family, then friends. Each round builds confidence.
👁️ 3. Make Eye Contact
Find 3 friendly faces in the room and move between them. It feels like conversation.
🐌 4. Slow Down
Nervous speakers rush. Pause after key points — it feels long to you, but sounds powerful to listeners.
💪 5. Power Pose
Stand tall with open shoulders for 2 minutes before speaking. It genuinely reduces anxiety!
🙌 6. Reframe It
Tell yourself "I'm excited!" instead of "I'm nervous." Your brain hears a different story.

Unit Thirteen
Media Literacy 📰
Reading the world around you with a critical, questioning eye
🌐Media literacy means being able to access, analyse, evaluate, and create media in all its forms. In a world of 24/7 news and social media, this skill is essential.
📺 5 Types of Media
📰 Print Media
Newspapers, magazines, books. Often edited and fact-checked — but can have a bias too!
📱 Social Media
Instagram, TikTok, X, YouTube. Fast and wide-reaching. Requires the most critical reading!
📡 Broadcast Media
TV, radio, podcasts. Depends on the station's editorial values and funding.
💻 Online Media
Websites, blogs, news apps. Quality varies enormously. Check the source carefully!
🎬 Visual Media
Films, documentaries, photography. Powerful — but images can also be edited or taken out of context.
📣 Advertising
Always has a purpose: to make you buy, believe, or do something. Always ask: who paid for this?
📰 Anatomy of a News Article
🗞️ Sample Article (Read & Analyse)
Young Scientists Win Global Prize for Ocean-Cleaning Robot
By A. Hassan | The World Times | 15 March 2026

A team of fourteen-year-old students from Lagos, Nigeria, has won the prestigious Global Youth Science Prize for their invention of "TideBot" — a solar-powered robot designed to collect microplastic waste from coastal waters.

The three-person team, led by Amara Osei, spent eight months building the prototype using recycled materials. "We wanted to solve a real problem in our community," said Osei in an interview. "Our beach was getting worse every year."

Scientists at the University of Lagos praised the invention as "impressive and practical." The team will now travel to Stockholm to present their work at the International Youth Forum in April.

Use these 4 steps before sharing anything you see online:

T

🚨 Red Flags: Is This Fake News?
⚠️ Warning Signs in Content
Headline uses SHOCKING or ALL CAPS language
Triggers very strong emotion (outrage, fear, joy)
No author name or publication date
No links to original data or research
Photos that look odd or taken out of context
Spelling errors or unusual domain name
✅ Signs of Reliable Content
Clear, calm headline (not sensational)
Named, qualified journalist or author
Date of publication clearly shown
Multiple sources cited and linked
Distinguishes between facts and opinions
Published by a known, established outlet

⬆️⬇️ Intonation — Rising or Falling?
⬆️ Rising Intonation
Used for: Yes/No questions, unfinished sentences, showing uncertainty.
Example: "Are you coming to the party tonight? ↗"
⬇️ Falling Intonation
Used for: Statements, Wh-questions, finished thoughts, commands.
Example: "She lives in London. ↘"

🔗 What Are Collocations?
A collocation is a pair or group of words that naturally go together in English. We say "make a decision" — NOT "do a decision." Learning collocations makes your English sound natural and fluent!
