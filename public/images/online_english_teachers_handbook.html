Introduction

Welcome to the Handbook

Online teaching is not simply teaching with a camera on. It is a distinct pedagogical practice that demands new skills, a redesigned relationship between teacher, learner, and content — and a willingness to rethink what a classroom can be.

Whether you are transitioning from a physical classroom, already established as an online teacher, or training others to teach in virtual environments, this handbook provides concrete, evidence-informed guidance across every dimension of online English language teaching.

Each section is designed to be read independently, so you can navigate directly to what you need. If you are new to online teaching, I recommend reading Part I in full before exploring methodology or strategy.

How to Use This Handbook

Use the sidebar to navigate between sections. Technique cards highlight specific, actionable strategies. Callout boxes signal key principles. Tool chips identify specific digital tools. This handbook is a living professional reference — return to it often as your practice evolves.

A Note on Pedagogical Stance

This handbook takes a learner-centred, communicative stance on language teaching. Every methodology and strategy presented here is grounded in applied linguistics, instructional design, and educational technology research. Where theory and practice diverge, practice wins — this guide leans hard toward what actually works in real online classrooms.

Part I

Foundations of Online English Teaching

Before methodologies and tools, there is mindset. Understanding the landscape of online teaching — its constraints, its freedoms, and its learners — is what separates effective online teachers from those who simply broadcast lessons through a screen.

Part I · Chapter 1

The Online Teaching Mindset

Effective online teaching begins long before the lesson starts. It begins with how you conceptualise your role, your relationship to technology, and your understanding of what learning looks like through a screen.

From Deliverer to Designer

In a physical classroom, a skilled teacher can improvise, read the room, redirect energy, and use proximity to manage behaviour and motivation. Online, that spontaneity narrows. What expands, however, is intentionality. The online teacher must design experiences with far more precision — because the environment offers fewer organic moments of connection.

Shift your self-concept from deliverer of content to designer of learning experiences. Every moment of a virtual lesson should be purposefully structured so that students are actively doing something: speaking, writing, reading, thinking, deciding, or creating.

Presence Over Performance
Core Principle

Online learners disengage when they sense the teacher is going through the motions. Genuine presence — looking into the camera, responding to what students actually say, showing curiosity and warmth — is more powerful than polished slides or elaborate activities.

Practice social presence: address students by name, acknowledge responses generously, and let your personality come through the screen.

Embrace Asynchronous Thinking
Mindset Shift

Many teachers default to thinking of online teaching as live lessons. But some of the most effective online learning happens outside the live session — in threaded discussions, video responses, collaborative documents, and self-paced tasks.

Consider what belongs live (interaction, debate, feedback, community) versus what can happen asynchronously (input, reading, vocabulary, writing drafts).

Digital Literacy as Professional Skill
Teacher Development

You do not need to master every tool. You need to confidently use a small core toolkit, know where to find new tools when a specific need arises, and evaluate new technologies critically before adopting them.

Digital literacy is not about being a tech expert. It is about purposeful, confident, and critical use of technology in service of learning.

The Five C Framework
Framework

Effective online teachers consistently demonstrate five qualities: Clarity (clear objectives, clear instructions), Connection (building relationships through the screen), Consistency (predictable routines learners rely on), Creativity (varied, engaging lesson design), and Care (genuine investment in learner progress).

Teaching online is not a downgrade from the physical classroom. It is a different — and in many ways more demanding — form of teaching that rewards intentionality above all else.

— Rayene Medabis, M.Ed., TESOL
Part I · Chapter 2

Understanding Your Digital Learners

Every learner who joins your virtual classroom brings a different relationship with technology, a different home environment, different motivations, and a different tolerance for the frustrations of online learning. Understanding this diversity is the first step toward meeting it.

What Online Learners Struggle With
  • Maintaining focus without physical classroom cues
  • Feeling isolated or disconnected from peers
  • Managing their own time in self-paced elements
  • Technical anxiety and troubleshooting
  • Balancing online learning with home life demands
  • Speaking up in virtual spaces, especially cameras off
  • Processing complex instructions without body language
What Online Learners Gain
  • Greater autonomy and self-direction
  • Access to authentic digital content and English media
  • Flexibility around their schedule and personal pace
  • Reduced anxiety from smaller digital interaction spaces
  • A permanent record of shared work and written feedback
  • Access to global English-speaking communities online
  • Development of real digital communication skills

Motivation in the Online Context

Motivation behaves differently online. Without the social energy of a physical classroom and without the accountability of showing up somewhere in person, learners require more intentional motivational scaffolding from their teacher.

Apply self-determination theory: support learners' sense of autonomy (give choices), competence (build in visible wins), and relatedness (cultivate genuine community). These three drivers, deliberately addressed in lesson design, produce significantly more motivated online learners.

Key Insight

The most common reason students disengage from online English classes is not the difficulty of the content — it is the feeling of invisibility. Design every lesson so that no learner can disappear into the background. Name them, prompt them, include them.

Setting Up Your Virtual Classroom

Your virtual classroom is a combination of the platform you teach on, the tools that extend its functionality, and the physical environment you teach from. All three shape the learning experience your students receive.

Your Physical Setup Matters

Camera & Framing

Position your camera at eye level — not looking up (impersonal) or looking down (creates power imbalance). Frame yourself so your face and upper chest are visible. Look into the camera lens, not at yourself on screen, to create genuine eye contact with students.

Lighting

Light from the front — natural window light or a ring light facing you. Avoid strong backlight (a window behind you creates a silhouette). Good lighting signals professionalism and makes your facial expressions readable, which learners rely on for teaching cues.

Audio

Poor audio is the single biggest barrier to learning in virtual classrooms. Use a dedicated microphone or quality headset. Minimise background noise. Test audio before every session. Learners can compensate for imperfect video — they cannot compensate for unclear speech.

Background & Space

A clean, uncluttered background keeps learner attention on you. If using a virtual background, ensure your camera supports it cleanly. Small personal touches — a plant, a bookshelf — humanise the space and give students something to connect with you over.

Part II

Core Methodologies for Online ELT

The approaches that underpin effective English language teaching all translate to online contexts — but each requires thoughtful adaptation. This section walks through the major methodologies and their practical application in virtual classrooms.

Part II · Chapter 4

Communicative Language Teaching Online

Communicative Language Teaching (CLT) remains the most widely endorsed approach to English language instruction — and it is entirely achievable in virtual environments. The shift requires rethinking what "communication" looks like on screen.

Principles of CLT in Virtual Spaces

CLT is built on the belief that language is learned through use — through authentic, purposeful communication rather than form-focused drills. Online, this means creating conditions for students to actually communicate: negotiate meaning, express opinions, solve problems, and interact with peers and real-world content in English.

Digital Role Plays
CLT Strategy

Assign roles via breakout rooms or private chat. Students take on personas (customer/agent, journalist/interviewee, doctor/patient) and communicate through audio or video. Digital role plays often reduce anxiety for reticent speakers who feel protected by the screen distance.

Tools: Zoom breakout rooms, Google Docs for written scenario cards

Information Gap Activities
CLT Strategy

Send different information to each student via private chat or separate shared documents. Students must communicate to complete the picture. This creates genuine communicative need — the engine of CLT — which is easy to engineer in a digital environment.

Tools: Google Docs, Zoom private chat, LMS for pre-session distribution

Authentic Text Discussion
CLT Strategy

Use real online content — news articles, social media posts, video clips, podcasts — as discussion springboards. Authentic materials bring real language and cultural context into the virtual classroom, and are just a browser tab away when you teach online.

Tools: Screen share, Padlet for response collection, embedded video

Structured Speaking Tasks
CLT Strategy

Use timed formats: spot-the-difference (share two similar images), picture descriptions, mini debates, opinion roundtables, or "hot seat" question activities. Clear structure and time limits reduce paralysis while the communicative goal drives student output.

Tools: Jamboard, Canva for visuals, Zoom countdown timer

Part II · Chapter 5

Task-Based Language Teaching

Task-Based Language Teaching (TBLT) organises instruction around meaningful tasks that mirror real-world language use. Online, TBLT gains a significant advantage: access to genuinely authentic digital tasks that students will encounter in their actual English-language lives.

The TBLT Lesson Cycle

1

Pre-Task

Introduce the topic and task. Activate prior knowledge. Provide necessary vocabulary support without pre-teaching everything. In an online context, this can include a short video introduction, a Padlet brainstorm, or a shared Google Doc with stimulus material and guiding questions.

2

Task Cycle

Students attempt the task in pairs or groups via breakout rooms. Monitor by moving between rooms. Resist the urge to correct during the task — the goal is fluency and meaning-making. Note recurring errors quietly for later focus during the language stage.

3

Planning & Report

Groups prepare to report back to the class. This planning phase naturally raises attention to accuracy. Online, students can prepare a shared Google Slide, a Padlet post, or a short recorded video summary of their work.

4

Language Focus

After the task, analyse the language used and needed. Address errors noted during monitoring. Use a shared annotation document or a Google Doc to highlight real language from the task — anchoring correction to what students actually produced creates lasting relevance.

TBLT Task Ideas for Online Classes

Plan a virtual trip using real travel websites. Write and record a product review. Compose a formal email to a real organisation. Create an explainer video on a topic of choice. Research and present a real news story. Design a social media campaign for a cause they care about. Conduct an interview with a partner and report back.

Part II · Chapter 6

The Flipped Classroom

The flipped model inverts the traditional lesson structure: input happens before the live session, and live time is used exclusively for interaction, practice, and production. Online, this model is especially powerful because pre-session materials are easy to distribute, track, and revisit.

Before the Session (Student Task)
  • Watch a short teacher-made or curated video (5–12 min)
  • Complete a vocabulary pre-task or quiz
  • Read a short text with guiding questions
  • Submit a brief reflection or Google Form quiz
  • Note questions and confusions to bring to the session
During the Live Session (Teacher Focus)
  • Clarify questions from pre-session work
  • Facilitate application and communication tasks
  • Run peer discussion, debate, and collaborative tasks
  • Provide targeted feedback and error correction
  • Extend understanding through interaction and reflection

Tools for Flipping

Loom — record and share video lessons EdPuzzle — embed questions into videos Screencastify — screen recording with annotation Google Forms — pre-session comprehension checks YouTube (unlisted) — share video content privately Nearpod — interactive async lesson delivery
Part II · Chapter 7

Blended Learning

Blended learning combines structured asynchronous content with live synchronous sessions to create a learning experience that is greater than the sum of its parts. It offers learner flexibility while preserving the irreplaceable human element of live teaching.

Self-Paced Modules
Async Component

Deliver grammar explanations, vocabulary sets, listening exercises, and reading tasks as structured modules on an LMS. Students complete these at their own pace, freeing live sessions entirely for communication, practice, and genuine human interaction.

Live Application Sessions
Sync Component

Use live sessions exclusively for what requires human interaction: discussions, role plays, speaking practice, peer feedback, Q&A, and community building. Guard this time from being diluted by content delivery that could happen asynchronously.

Progress Tracking
Management

In blended models, tracking completion and progress is essential. Use LMS analytics to identify who is falling behind. Reach out proactively — a brief personalised message noticing a student's absence from modules often re-engages them before they disengage completely.

Part II · Chapter 8

Project-Based Learning

Project-Based Learning (PBL) engages learners in sustained, meaningful investigation of real-world questions or challenges. In online English teaching, PBL produces some of the most authentic and memorable language use of any approach.

Anatomy of an Online PBL Unit

1

Driving Question

Launch with a compelling, open question. Examples: "How can we use English to explain our culture to the world?" or "What makes a great product, and how do companies convince people to buy it?" The question should be genuinely answerable in multiple valid ways.

2

Research & Input Phase

Students gather information, read, watch, and discuss. Provide structured scaffolding through graphic organisers and guided note-taking on shared Google Docs. Live sessions during this phase focus on the specific language the project demands.

3

Creation Phase

Students produce a tangible output: a presentation, video, podcast, report, or website. Collaborative tools like Canva, Google Slides, or Padlet make digital co-creation accessible, trackable, and genuinely engaging for modern learners.

4

Public Presentation

Share work with an audience beyond the classroom — post to a class blog, present to another group, share on a class Padlet wall. Authentic audience drives quality and purpose. Even in a small online class, showcasing work publicly raises the stakes in motivating ways.

5

Structured Reflection

End every project unit with structured reflection — on language learned, skills developed, and process navigated. Use written journals, video responses on Flip, or a live debrief discussion. Reflection transforms experience into learning.

Part II · Chapter 9

CLIL Online

Content and Language Integrated Learning (CLIL) teaches subject matter through the medium of English, developing both content knowledge and language skills simultaneously. Online, CLIL works exceptionally well because authentic English-medium content is just a browser tab away.

CLIL in Practice Online

Use real English-language science articles, BBC documentaries, TED Talks, or National Geographic content as both language input and subject matter. Language becomes a vehicle for genuine intellectual engagement — which is precisely what CLIL intends, and what online environments make effortlessly accessible.

Language Scaffolds for CLIL
  • Glossaries and visual vocabulary banks per topic
  • Sentence frames for academic discussion and analysis
  • Graphic organisers for content comprehension
  • Pre-teaching key technical vocabulary before content
  • Bilingual support where appropriate and contextual
Suitable Content Areas Online
  • Science & environment (BBC, National Geographic)
  • History & culture (Smithsonian, Khan Academy)
  • Business & economics (FT, Harvard Business Review)
  • Technology (Wired, MIT Technology Review)
  • Social issues (TED Talks, Vox, Al Jazeera English)
Part III

Engagement & Interaction

Engagement is the central challenge of online English teaching. It is not a bonus — it is the precondition for learning. This section covers synchronous and asynchronous engagement strategies and the digital tools that make them possible.

Part III · Chapter 10

Synchronous Engagement Strategies

Live sessions are precious. Their power lies in real-time human interaction — the exchange that no asynchronous activity can fully replicate. Design every minute of live time to demand and reward active participation.

The First Five Minutes

The opening of an online lesson sets the tone for everything that follows. A weak opening leads to passive learners. A strong opening activates thinking, establishes energy, and signals that this session will demand genuine participation.

The Chat Warm-Up

Post a low-stakes question in the chat as students arrive: "Type one word to describe your week" or "Would you rather live in the city or countryside? Drop your answer." This normalises chat participation and warms students up before the lesson proper begins.

Live Word Cloud

Open Mentimeter or Poll Everywhere and display a live word cloud as students type their responses to a prompt. Visual, immediate, and genuinely engaging — word clouds collectivise the class experience and create a shared artefact before the lesson content begins.

Mystery Image Hook

Share a partially revealed image (zoomed in, blurred, or cropped) and ask students to speculate. "What do you think this is? Where is it? What might be happening?" Simple, visual, and immediately communicative. Gradually reveal the full image as students guess and interact.

The Committed Chat Response

Display a thought-provoking question. Give students 60 seconds to type their response before anyone speaks aloud. "Everyone type your answer — hold it — 3, 2, 1, send!" This democratises participation: every student commits before the conversation narrows around vocal students.

Breakout Rooms: Best Practices

1

Give Explicit Instructions Before Breaking Out

State the task, the time limit, the expected output, and what happens at the end. Share instructions in the chat before sending students to breakout rooms. No student should enter a breakout room uncertain of what they are doing.

2

Assign a Shared Workspace

Give each group a shared Google Doc, Padlet card, or Jamboard sticky note area. This externalises the task, provides a visible record of their work, and gives you something concrete to reference during the debrief afterwards.

3

Visit Every Room

Move through breakout rooms to monitor progress, answer questions, and note language use. Keep visits brief — 2–3 minutes per room. Your presence signals that the task is real and that you are actively invested in what is happening.

4

Debrief Meaningfully

Collect reports purposefully — not by going through every group sequentially, but by asking for highlights, points of disagreement, or surprising findings. Make the debrief a genuine conversation, not an administrative round-up.

Energisers & Brain Breaks

Online fatigue is neurologically real. Schedule a 3–5 minute break every 45–50 minutes of instruction. Use the break as a language opportunity, not just a pause — a quick game, a fun prompt, or a movement activity that keeps English at the centre.

Part III · Chapter 11

Asynchronous Engagement Strategies

Asynchronous tasks extend learning beyond the live session, build learner autonomy, and allow students to engage with content at their own pace — especially important for adult, working, and multilingual learners managing competing demands.

Video Response Tasks
Speaking Async

Use Flip (formerly Flipgrid) to assign short speaking tasks between sessions. Students record themselves responding to a prompt and watch classmates' responses. This creates genuine peer interaction and authentic speaking practice without requiring synchronous presence.

Example: "Tell us about a time you had to use English outside of class. What happened?"

Discussion Boards
Writing Async

Post a discussion question on your LMS or Padlet. Require students to post an initial response and reply to at least two classmates. Set clear expectations: minimum word count, evidence-based responses, respectful engagement. Discussion boards also build academic writing skills.

Audio Journaling
Speaking Async

Assign weekly audio journal entries using Vocaroo or SpeakPipe. Students record a 2–3 minute reflection on the week's learning or a prompt you set. You respond with a personal audio note — far more human than a written comment, and far more memorable.

Digital Portfolios
Showcase

Have students build a digital portfolio throughout the course — a running collection of their best work, reflections, and evidence of progress. Use Google Sites, Seesaw (young learners), or Notion. Portfolios make learning visible, motivate sustained effort, and serve as powerful summative evidence.

Part III · Chapter 12

Digital Tools Deep Dive

The right tool, used at the right moment for the right purpose, can transform a flat lesson into a dynamic one. The wrong tool — adopted because it is new or because a colleague mentioned it — adds cognitive load without adding value.

The Tool Adoption Principle

Before adopting any new digital tool, ask: Does this serve a pedagogical purpose I cannot meet as effectively without it? If the answer is no, set it aside. The best online teachers use fewer tools with greater depth, rather than a rotating carousel of novelties that disorients learners.

Part IV

Teaching Across Levels & Ages

The principles of good online teaching apply across the age and proficiency spectrum — but their expression varies enormously. What works for a 7-year-old beginner will not work for a C1 adult professional, and vice versa.

Part IV · Chapter 13

Teaching Young Learners Online

Young learners (approximately ages 4–12) bring enormous energy, imagination, and willingness to engage — but their short attention spans, developing digital literacy, and need for physical movement make online teaching a genuine design challenge that rewards creativity.

Short, Varied Activities
YL Design

Plan activities of 5–8 minutes maximum for young learners (shorter for very young learners). Switch between modalities — listening, speaking, drawing, moving, singing — to sustain attention across a session. Aim for 6–8 distinct activities per hour of instruction.

TPR Adapted for Screens
YL Methodology

Total Physical Response (TPR) works beautifully online. Instruct students to stand up, point, jump, draw, or act out vocabulary. Physical response anchors language in the body even through a screen, and the kinaesthetic engagement reduces restlessness in young learners.

Story-Based Learning
YL Strategy

Structure lessons around a story. Use digital picture books (share your screen), puppet characters, or recurring story characters to create narrative continuity. Young learners engage deeply with story and remember language contextualised within it far longer than isolated items.

Resources: Storyline Online, Epic! Books, Oxford Reading Tree Digital

Songs, Chants & Rhymes
YL Strategy

Music and rhythm are among the most powerful supports for young language learners. Share audio or video, invite students to sing and move along. Songs make vocabulary sticky, lessons memorable, and the session joyful — which is itself a pedagogical value worth defending.

Involving Parents in Young Learner Online Classes

For very young learners (ages 4–7), communicate expectations to parents clearly before the course begins. Brief parents on the week's vocabulary so they can reinforce it at home. A simple weekly parent message via WhatsApp or email — "This week we practised: ..." — dramatically extends learning beyond the screen and builds strong home-school communication.

Part IV · Chapter 14

Teaching Teenagers Online

Teenagers are often the most challenging online learners — and with the right approach, the most rewarding. The key is designing for their need for relevance, autonomy, peer connection, and the opportunity to express their own identities through the language.

Make It Genuinely Relevant
Teen Engagement

Connect language tasks to things teenagers actually care about: music, gaming, social media, identity, justice, relationships, and future plans. "Analyse this song's lyrics for persuasive language" produces more engagement than "read this paragraph about shopping trends."

Social Media Formats
Teen Strategy

Frame writing and speaking tasks in formats teenagers know intimately: write an Instagram caption, record a TikTok-style explainer, create a Twitter thread on a news story, or design a podcast intro. Familiar formats reduce resistance to producing in English.

Maximise Peer Collaboration
Teen Strategy

Teenagers are social learners. Maximise peer interaction in breakout rooms. Use peer feedback structures with clear rubrics. Collaborative creative projects — co-written stories, group podcasts, shared presentations — tap into the social energy that is the primary driver of adolescent learning.

Offer Meaningful Choices
Teen Autonomy

Provide task format choices where possible: "You can write a letter, record a short video, or create a visual presentation for the same task." Meeting the same language objective while honouring different preferences and strengths is one of the most effective teen engagement strategies available.

Part IV · Chapter 15

Teaching Adult Learners Online

Adult learners bring decades of life experience, self-directedness, and clear purpose to their English learning. They are often time-poor and highly motivated — a combination that demands both efficiency and deep respect for their existing competence.

Andragogy: The Core Framework for Adult Learning

Malcolm Knowles' principles of adult learning remind us that adults need to know why they are learning something; bring rich self-concept and experience to the classroom; are ready to learn what is directly relevant to their real-life situations; are problem-centred rather than content-centred; and are intrinsically motivated. Design every adult lesson around these truths.

What Adults Need Online
  • Clear, stated objectives at the start of every session
  • Efficient, purposeful use of their limited time
  • Direct connection between tasks and real-life use
  • Respect for their existing knowledge and experience
  • Flexible asynchronous options within the course
  • Professional and business-relevant content where appropriate
  • Direct, constructive, specific feedback
What to Avoid with Adults
  • Infantilising tasks or childlike visual design
  • Excessive games without clear language purpose
  • Over-scaffolding what they can already accomplish
  • Vague encouragement instead of genuine feedback
  • Ignoring their specific, stated language goals
  • Rigid synchronous-only schedules with no flexibility
  • One-size-fits-all content that ignores professional diversity
Part IV · Chapter 16

Teaching Advanced Learners Online

Advanced learners (C1–C2) often arrive with strong passive competence but limited exposure to high-level productive use. Online teaching at this level is about pushing the edges of fluency, accuracy, and complexity — and creating genuine intellectual challenge.

Seminar-Style Discussion
Advanced Strategy

Structure live sessions as seminars: assign readings or podcasts in advance, designate student discussion leaders, require evidence-based contributions. Hold students to C1–C2 standards of accuracy, sophistication, and coherence — and name it clearly and specifically when they hit it.

Research & Presentation Projects
Advanced Strategy

Assign independent research projects where learners investigate a topic, synthesise multiple English-language sources, and present findings in an academic format. Focus feedback on precision of expression, appropriate register, and the coherence of argument structure.

Extensive Reading & Listening
Input

Assign authentic long-form reading and listening: novels, longform journalism, documentary series, academic lectures. Advanced learners need massive comprehensible input at genuine English-language level. Online teaching makes curating and distributing this input remarkably easy.

Stylistic Writing Workshops
Writing

Workshop advanced writing: analyse a model text stylistically, identify the techniques the writer uses, then write a piece applying those same techniques. Focus feedback on voice, register, precision, and argument. Share drafts on a collaborative Google Doc for peer and teacher annotation.

Part V

Assessment & Feedback

Assessment drives learning. Feedback shapes improvement. In online contexts, both require intentional adaptation to remain as powerful and meaningful as they are in physical classrooms.

Part V · Chapter 17

Formative Assessment Online

Formative assessment is assessment for learning — the ongoing gathering of evidence about where learners are, used to adjust teaching in real time. Online, this requires deliberate design because the classroom cues that alert teachers to confusion are harder to read on a screen.

Exit Tickets
Formative Tool

End every session with a 2–3 question exit ticket via Google Forms or Mentimeter. Ask what they learned, what they are still unsure about, and one question they have. Review responses before the next session and open by addressing what students raised.

Reaction Buttons Check
Formative Tool

Use Zoom's reaction buttons or ask students to physically show thumbs up (understood), thumbs sideways (mostly understood), or thumbs down (confused). Simple, fast, and surprisingly informative — especially when you ask follow-up questions to students who signal uncertainty.

Simultaneous Chat Checks
Formative Tool

Ask a specific comprehension question and have students answer in the chat simultaneously. "Everyone type the answer before you send it — 3, 2, 1, send!" ensures genuine individual checking, avoids the "Any questions?" silence, and gives you whole-class data instantly.

Shared Whiteboard Responses
Formative Tool

Ask students to write answers, diagram ideas, or map concepts on a shared Jamboard or Miro board. You see all responses simultaneously. This surfaces misunderstanding instantly and produces rich data about where the class is — both collectively and individually.

Part V · Chapter 18

Summative Assessment Online

Summative assessment evaluates learning at the end of a unit, course, or learning period. Online, assessment design must balance rigour with the reality that students complete assessments in unsupervised environments — which changes how we design for validity.

High-Integrity Assessment Designs
  • Performance tasks — demonstrate ability by doing
  • Video presentations — record and submit speaking work
  • Portfolio submissions — curated evidence of development
  • Live speaking assessments — via individual video call
  • Process writing — drafts submitted alongside final piece
  • Project outputs — multimedia or written final products
Managing Academic Integrity
  • Design tasks requiring personalised, unique response
  • Include a reflective element that demands genuine thought
  • Use live components alongside written components
  • Set submissions that draw on in-class work students completed
  • Use oral confirmation of written submissions where needed
  • Build relationships — students cheat less for teachers they trust
Part V · Chapter 19

Error Correction Online

Error correction in online teaching requires the same principled decision-making as in physical classrooms — but the channels and timing look different. How you correct, when you correct, and who sees the correction all matter enormously for learner confidence.

Delayed Board Correction
Live Session

Note errors during speaking tasks and write them on a shared whiteboard after the task ends. Ask students to identify and correct the errors themselves before you intervene. Always anonymise errors — never connect a mistake to a specific student publicly during a session.

Private Chat Correction
Live Session

For sensitive errors, use private chat to correct a student discreetly. This is especially effective in adult and professional contexts where public correction can cause real embarrassment. Type: "Just to note — it's 'I have been working here' not 'I am working here since three years.'"

Google Docs Annotation
Written Feedback

Use Google Docs' comment and suggestion features for written error correction. Rather than simply correcting, ask questions in comments: "Is this the right tense here? What time frame are you describing?" Prompting self-correction before providing the answer produces deeper learning.

Error Pattern Lessons
Teaching from Data

Collect recurring errors across multiple students and turn them into a focused mini-lesson. Open the next session with: "I noticed many of us are using X this way — let's look at it together." This normalises error, makes correction a shared event, and feels collaborative rather than corrective.

Part V · Chapter 20

Feedback Strategies

Feedback is one of the most powerful influences on learning. In online contexts, it must work harder to compensate for the absence of spontaneous in-class encouragement and the physical presence of a teacher who clearly cares.

The Feedback Quality Ladder

1

Evaluative Feedback (Weakest)

"Good job!" or "This needs work." Tells the student nothing actionable about what is working or what to change. Avoid this as your primary feedback form — it is noise, not signal.

2

Descriptive Feedback

"Your argument in paragraph two is well-supported by evidence." Describes what is observed but still does not guide next steps — better than evaluative, but not yet sufficient.

3

Suggestive Feedback

"Consider adding a counter-argument in paragraph three to strengthen your overall position." Provides clear direction for improvement — significantly more useful and actionable than descriptive alone.

4

Dialogic Feedback (Strongest)

Feedback delivered as a conversation — via Loom video, audio note, or a brief live meeting — that invites the student to respond and participate in their own improvement process. This is the gold standard of online feedback, and the most memorable for learners.

Part VI

Differentiation & Inclusion

Every online class contains a range of learners — in readiness, background, learning style, and need. Effective online teachers design for the range, not the middle. Differentiation is not an extra; it is the work.

Part VI · Chapter 21

Meeting Diverse Needs Online

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) provides a practical framework for differentiation in online teaching. It asks teachers to provide multiple means of representation, engagement, and expression — so all learners can access content and demonstrate learning.

UDL PrincipleWhat It MeansOnline Application
Multiple Means of Representation Present information in more than one format Use video + audio + text for key content; provide transcripts; use visual organisers alongside written instructions
Multiple Means of Engagement Provide different ways to connect with learning Offer task choices; vary group sizes; allow self-paced work; connect to real-world learner interests and goals
Multiple Means of Expression Allow students to demonstrate learning in different ways Accept video, audio, written, or visual responses; offer project-based alternatives to traditional assessment
Part VI · Chapter 22

Scaffolding Online

Scaffolding provides temporary support that enables learners to achieve more than they could independently. Online, scaffolds can be built directly into digital materials — available on demand, rather than delivered improvisationally in the moment.

Language Frames
Scaffold Type

Provide sentence starters and frames for speaking and writing tasks: "In my opinion, ... because ..."; "This text suggests that ..."; "I disagree with ... because ..." Share these in the chat or on a visible slide during tasks so students can reference them without interrupting their flow.

Tiered Tasks
Scaffold Type

Design the same task at three levels: supported (vocabulary bank and sentence frames), standard (guiding questions only), and extended (open-ended with additional challenge). Assign levels sensitively or allow student choice with a transparent rationale about purpose.

Visual Organisers
Scaffold Type

Graphic organisers, mind maps, and structured templates reduce cognitive load by externalising the thinking structure. Distribute via Google Slides or Jamboard before the task begins. Canva has excellent, customisable templates for every age group and task type.

Vocabulary Banks
Scaffold Type

Provide a pinned or shared vocabulary resource for each lesson or unit — a Google Doc, Quizlet set, or LMS glossary. Include pronunciation guides, examples in context, and translation where appropriate for lower levels. Make it searchable and permanently accessible.

Part VII

Virtual Classroom Management

Management in a virtual classroom is not about control — it is about creating conditions in which learning can happen. That means clear structures, predictable routines, and proactive management of participation and community culture.

Part VII · Chapter 23

Virtual Norms & Classroom Culture

The culture of your virtual classroom is established in the first two sessions and reinforced every session thereafter. Invest time at the start of a course in co-creating norms — students who help write the rules are far more likely to honour them.

Co-Creating Classroom Norms

In your first session, ask students: "What makes an online lesson work well for you?" Collect responses on a shared Padlet or Jamboard. Group ideas into categories (participation, technology, respect, punctuality) and formalise them as a shared class agreement. Return to this agreement when norms are tested — and they will be.

Common Virtual Classroom Norms
  • Join on time with your displayed name correct
  • Mute your microphone when not speaking
  • Use the chat respectfully and on-topic
  • Signal via reaction buttons if you have a tech problem
  • Keep camera on during discussions where possible
  • Be fully present — no parallel tasks during live sessions
  • English as the communication language during class time
Camera Policy Considerations
  • Cameras off should never be punished — there are often valid reasons
  • Instead, create conditions where students want to turn cameras on
  • Breakout rooms have higher camera-on rates than main sessions
  • Acknowledge camera-on students naturally and warmly
  • Respect privacy concerns, home environments, and bandwidth
  • For formal assessments, camera-on is a justifiable requirement
Part VII · Chapter 24

Managing Participation Online

In a physical classroom, silent students are visible. Online, they can disappear entirely. Proactive participation management ensures no learner remains invisible — and that the most vocal students do not dominate at the expense of quieter ones.

Randomised Calling
Participation Tool

Use Wheel of Names or ClassDojo to randomly select students to respond. Make this a positive, low-stakes ritual from the start of the course so students expect to be called and prepare accordingly. Always pair randomised calling with adequate thinking time before speaking.

Simultaneous Chat Responses
Participation Tool

Have everyone type their answer and hold it before sending. "3, 2, 1 — send!" Every student commits simultaneously. You get whole-class data instantly, and no student can wait to copy a more vocal peer's response — one of the most powerful simple techniques in online teaching.

Partner Accountability Pairs
Participation Structure

Assign stable "online partners" at the start of the course. Partners are responsible for each other's understanding and progress. "Check with your partner" becomes a consistent routine. When a student is absent, their partner notices — and that noticing is itself a powerful motivation.

The Private Check-In
Wellbeing & Presence

Send quieter students a brief private chat message during or after sessions: "How are you finding the material this week?" This opens a channel beyond the live session and signals that you notice their presence as an individual — not just as a participant count.

Part VII · Chapter 25

Handling Technical Difficulties

Technology will fail. Internet will drop. Platforms will crash. Microphones will refuse to cooperate at the worst possible moment. The mark of a skilled online teacher is not that technology never fails — it is how gracefully they handle it when it does.

The Tech Failure Protocol

Establish a clear protocol at the start of every course: "If you lose connection, rejoin via the same link. If I lose connection, I will rejoin within three minutes. If neither of us can reconnect, check your email or our WhatsApp group for instructions on how we will continue." Students who know what to do during a tech failure experience far less anxiety when it happens.

Backup Communication Channel

Always maintain a secondary channel: a WhatsApp group, email list, or LMS announcement space. If the main platform goes down, students know exactly where to check. Send the day's task or a holding activity immediately upon experiencing a technical failure.

Autonomous Task Ready

Keep a shared Google Doc or LMS page with an independent task always prepared and accessible. If you lose connection mid-lesson, students continue working independently. When you return, debrief what they completed. A tech failure becomes a self-directed learning moment.

Stay Calm & Narrate

Narrate technical issues as they happen: "My screen share has frozen — I'm restarting it, give me 30 seconds." Students mirror teacher energy. If you are calm and solution-focused, they will be too. If you panic, the session derails. Model tech resilience explicitly and visibly.

Part VIII

Teacher Wellbeing & Professional Growth

Online teachers face particular risks of burnout, isolation, and the specific exhaustion that comes from spending long hours teaching through a screen. Wellbeing is not a luxury — it is a professional responsibility and a precondition for effective teaching.

Part VIII · Chapter 26

Avoiding Screen Fatigue & Burnout

Zoom fatigue is documented and neurologically grounded. Research identifies four primary causes: sustained close-up eye contact, the effort of self-monitoring on camera, reduced physical mobility, and the cognitive overhead of processing social cues without a body. Online teachers experience all four, simultaneously, for extended periods, repeatedly.

Schedule Camera-Off Blocks
Wellbeing Practice

During asynchronous student work or student-led breakout tasks, turn your own camera off. Intentionally step away from the screen for 2–3 minutes during independent work. You do not need to be visible on camera for every single moment of an online lesson.

Protect Gap Time Between Sessions
Wellbeing Practice

Protect at least 15–20 minutes between consecutive online sessions. Use this time away from your screen — make tea, move your body, look out a window. The lack of physical transition between lessons is one of the underacknowledged cumulative costs of teaching online full-time.

Batch Your Lesson Planning
Sustainability

Plan and prepare materials in batches, not lesson by lesson on the day before. Dedicate one focused block per week to planning the following week in full. This reduces the cognitive overhead of constant context-switching between teaching mode and preparation mode.

Recognise Burnout Early
Self-Awareness

Watch for these signs: dreading sessions you once enjoyed, declining lesson quality, resentment of students, persistent fatigue that sleep does not resolve, cynicism about the profession. These are signals to slow down, seek support, and adjust — not to push harder through them.

Part VIII · Chapter 27

Sustainable Online Teaching Practice

Sustainability means building systems and habits that allow you to teach well over the long term — not just brilliantly in the short term. Brilliance that burns out is not effective teaching; it is a temporary performance.

Build Your Digital Resource Library

Invest time in building a digital resource library: a folder structure in Google Drive or Notion where you store lesson materials, templates, activity ideas, and video links — organised by level, skill, age group, and theme. The upfront investment pays compound returns over months and years of online teaching.

Template your most used document types: feedback sheets, session plans, unit maps, student reports. A well-designed template saves significant time and ensures quality consistency across your work.

The Five-Minute Reflection Habit

After each session, spend five minutes noting: one thing that worked well, one thing to adjust next time, and one student who needs closer attention in the following session. Brief, consistent reflection over time produces more professional growth than any formal training programme — and is far more efficient than lengthy post-lesson analysis.

Part VIII · Chapter 28

Continuous Professional Development

The field of online English language teaching is evolving rapidly. Teachers who commit to ongoing professional learning remain effective, relevant, and energised by a field that is genuinely exciting and expanding in scope and influence.

Online Teacher Communities
  • IATEFL Online — global ELT professional community
  • TESOL International — leading professional association
  • iTDi — independent teacher development initiative
  • ELT Chat — real-time teacher Twitter discussions
  • Cambridge English Teacher — professional resources and CPD
  • British Council Teaching English — articles, webinars, forums
Professional Development Habits
  • Attend one webinar or online conference session per month
  • Read one ELT article or chapter per week
  • Maintain a teaching journal or short reflection log
  • Observe a peer's online lesson at least once per term
  • Try one new tool or activity format per month
  • Conduct an action research cycle at least once per year
Building Your Personal Learning Network (PLN)

A Personal Learning Network is your curated ecosystem of teachers, researchers, and educators who expand your thinking and challenge your practice. Build yours deliberately: follow ELT thought leaders on LinkedIn and X/Twitter, join teacher Facebook groups, participate actively in webinar Q&As, and be generous in sharing your own insights and resources publicly. What you contribute to a PLN is what makes it valuable in return.
