Getting Started

How to Use This Catalogue

This catalogue is designed as a ready-to-use asynchronous resource. Each assessment includes everything a teacher needs: student-facing tasks, visual supports, and a scoring rubric. No preparation required beyond selecting the right assessment for your learners.

📊 CEFR Levels Covered

A1
Beginner
Introduces themselves, names familiar objects, uses very basic phrases. Highly visual support provided.
A2
Elementary
Communicates in simple routines, describes surroundings and immediate needs in short sentences.
B1
Intermediate
Handles most everyday situations, describes experiences, gives reasons and explanations.
B2
Upper-Intermediate
Interacts with fluency, understands complex texts, produces clear detailed writing on many topics.

🗂 Assessment Types

🌱
Formative
Low-stakes, ongoing checks. Use mid-unit to guide instruction. Focus on progress over accuracy. Results inform next steps, not grades.
🎯
Summative
End-of-unit or term evaluations. Graded against rubrics. Demonstrate cumulative learning and readiness to progress to the next level.
⚡
Quiz
Quick knowledge checks (15–25 min). Focused on vocabulary, reading comprehension, and specific grammar. Can be used as warm-ups or diagnostic tools.

🗣️SpeakingAsync recording prompts, scenario responses, oral narratives. Students record audio or video independently.
📖ReadingAuthentic texts, visual tasks, inference & comprehension. Ranges from picture matching to critical analysis.
✏️WritingTask-based writing in authentic formats: postcards, diaries, emails, essays, and professional portfolios.

My World Poster Talk

Students describe a personal poster about their world — family, home, and favourite things.

A1A2B1B2
📋 Teacher Notes

Before the task: Send students the poster template (or let them draw freely on paper) at least one day before recording. The poster has 4 sections: My Family, My Home, My Favourite Things, My Friends.

Task delivery: Students record themselves talking about their completed poster. Encourage them to point to each section as they speak.

Adaptation (lower): Provide sentence starters on screen. Adaptation (higher): Challenge students to describe using full sentences without starters.

Max's Lucky Day
A short story — approx. 150 words
Max woke up late for school. He ran to the kitchen, but there was no time for breakfast. He grabbed his bag and ran to the bus stop — but the bus was already driving away!

Max felt terrible. He walked slowly to school, thinking about how to explain being late to his teacher. On the way, he looked down and saw something shiny on the ground. It was a coin! Then he saw another one. And another!

When Max arrived at school, he showed his teacher the coins. "Class," said the teacher, "Max found something interesting today. Let's learn about old money!"

The whole class gathered around Max's coins. The teacher said they might be valuable. Max smiled. Being late wasn't so bad after all. Sometimes, the slow path takes you to the best places.

Photo Story Challenge

Students connect three seemingly unrelated photos into an original spoken narrative — testing creativity and spontaneous language production.

A1A2B1B2
📋 Teacher Notes

Beyond traditional: Unlike a standard picture description, students must invent a story that connects all three images. This assesses creative thinking, narrative structure, and spontaneous language production simultaneously.

Setup: Share the 3 photos with students. Give them 5 minutes of silent planning time. They may write bullet-point notes only — no full sentences in preparation.

A1A2B1B2

Beyond traditional: Unlike traditional speaking tasks, this assesses argumentative competence — the ability to hold a position, support it with reasoning, acknowledge a counterpoint, and still conclude clearly. This is a key B1–B2 speaking skill.

Assessment focus: Logical structure of argument, not just language fluency. A student with grammatical errors but a well-structured argument should score well on content.

Differentiation: B1 students may use the structure guide provided. B2 students should aim to argue without using the guide, showing natural discourse flow.

📐 Your Argument Structure
STEP 1
State your position — Do you agree or disagree? Say clearly.
"I strongly believe that…" / "I disagree with this statement because…"
STEP 2
Give Reason 1 with an example or statistic.
"Firstly… For example…"
STEP 3
Give Reason 2 with an example.
"Furthermore / In addition…"
STEP 4
Counter-argument + rebuttal — Acknowledge the other side, then respond.
"Some people argue that… However…"
STEP 5
Conclusion — Restate your position firmly.
"In conclusion, I firmly believe that…"

Are Screens Stealing Our Sleep?
Opinion piece · Youth Health Review · approx. 320 words
Walk into any teenager's bedroom after 10 p.m. and you will almost certainly find the same scene: a phone, a laptop, or both, glowing in the darkness. It is a picture that has become so familiar that we barely question it. But perhaps we should.

The evidence is clear. Research from the Sleep Foundation suggests that teenagers who use screens in the hour before bed take, on average, significantly longer to fall asleep than those who don't. Blue light, emitted by screens, suppresses melatonin — the hormone that tells your body it is time to sleep. The result? Teenagers are going to bed later, waking up more tired, and performing worse in school. This is not a minor inconvenience. This is a public health issue.

Defenders of screen time will argue that phones are simply modern tools — no different from the books teenagers once read before bed. This is, frankly, a misleading comparison. Books do not send notifications. Books do not algorithmically serve you content designed to keep your attention for one more minute, and then another, and then another.

Some schools have begun banning phones entirely. While this is an understandable reaction, it is not the answer. The goal should not be to eliminate technology — it should be to teach teenagers how to manage it. Sleep hygiene education, digital detox challenges, and parental involvement in setting device limits are all evidence-based approaches that have shown real results.

The screens are not going away. But our silence about their impact on young people's sleep absolutely should.

The Remote Work Revolution: Are We Ready?
Opinion piece · Contemporary Work Review · approx. 420 words

[Paragraph 1] Over the past five years, remote work has transformed from a rare privilege into an everyday reality for millions of workers worldwide. What began as an emergency response to disruption has evolved into a permanent fixture of the modern workplace. But as organisations navigate this seismic shift, a critical question emerges: is the remote work revolution genuinely liberating, or are we trading one set of problems for another?

[Paragraph 2] Proponents of remote work celebrate its obvious advantages. Workers reclaim hours previously lost to commuting. Parents can attend school events without requesting special permission. Individuals with disabilities gain access to opportunities that physical offices denied them. Research suggests that remote workers show productivity gains of approximately 13 percent compared to their office-based counterparts. These are not trivial benefits.

[Paragraph 3] Yet the picture is more complicated. For many workers — particularly those in smaller homes or with young children — the boundary between professional and personal life has dissolved entirely. Studies suggest that remote workers log an average of two additional hours per day, blurring into evenings and weekends. "Flexibility," it seems, can become a polished word for "always available."

[Paragraph 4] There are deeper concerns too. The informal learning that happens naturally in an office — watching a senior colleague handle a difficult client, absorbing the culture of an organisation through daily interaction — is difficult to replicate via video call. Junior employees may be disproportionately affected, entering careers without the mentoring relationships that once shaped professional development.

[Paragraph 5] The remote work debate ultimately reveals that there is no single model that suits everyone equally. The most thoughtful organisations are resisting the temptation of simple solutions and instead investing in the harder work: understanding what their people actually need, and building structures that honour the complexity of human lives. The remote work revolution is real. Whether it represents progress depends entirely on who is doing the measuring — and what they choose to count.
