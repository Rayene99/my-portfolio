The Tortoise and the Hare

Hare loved to brag about how fast he could run. One day he laughed at slow Tortoise and challenged him to a race. All the animals came to watch. When the race began, Hare zoomed far ahead. He was so far ahead that he stopped to nap under a shady tree. Tortoise did not stop. He walked on, one small step at a time. While Hare was sleeping, Tortoise passed him! Hare woke up and ran as fast as he could, but it was too late. Tortoise crossed the finish line first, and everyone cheered.

New Words
brag — to talk proudly about yourself
challenge — to ask someone to compete
zoomed — moved very fast
steady — moving in a calm, even way
Let's Think
  1. Who won the race?
  2. Why did Hare lose the race?
  3. What do you think Tortoise felt when he won?
Trace the Moral
Slow and steady wins the race.
Now You Write
Write about something you finished by not giving up.

The Boy Who Cried Wolf

A shepherd boy watched sheep on a hill. He felt bored, so he shouted, 'Wolf! Wolf!' The villagers ran to help, but there was no wolf at all. The boy laughed. He tricked them again the next day. The villagers were angry, but they still came running. Then one day, a real wolf came! The boy shouted for help, but no one believed him this time. No one came. The wolf scared away all the sheep. The boy learned that if you tell lies, people stop trusting you, even when you tell the truth.

New Words
shepherd — a person who takes care of sheep
villagers — people who live in a village
tricked — fooled someone
trust — to believe someone is honest
Let's Think
  1. What did the boy shout when there was no wolf?
  2. Why didn't the villagers come the last time?
  3. How can we be more trustworthy than the boy?
Trace the Moral
Always tell the truth.
Now You Write
Write one reason it is important to tell the truth.

The Ant and the Grasshopper

All summer, Ant worked hard. She carried food to her nest, bit by bit, to save for winter. Grasshopper sang and played in the sun all day. 'Why work so hard?' he asked. 'Come sing with me!' Ant just kept working. When winter came, snow covered the ground. Grasshopper had no food and was very hungry and cold. He knocked on Ant's door. Kind Ant shared her food with him. Grasshopper thanked her and promised to plan ahead next time.

New Words
harvest — to gather food or crops
prepare — to get ready for later
shivered — shook because of the cold
promised — said you will do something
Let's Think
  1. What did Ant do all summer?
  2. What happened to Grasshopper in winter?
  3. What lesson did Grasshopper learn?
Trace the Moral
It is wise to plan ahead.
Now You Write
Write about something you can do today to help your future self.

The Lion and the Mouse

A big lion was sleeping when a tiny mouse ran across his paw. The lion woke up and grabbed the mouse. 'Please let me go,' squeaked the mouse. 'One day I might help you.' The lion laughed, but he let her go anyway. Later, hunters trapped the lion in a net. He roared for help. The little mouse heard him and ran over. She chewed the ropes with her sharp teeth until the lion was free. 'Thank you, little friend,' said the lion. 'You were right after all.'

New Words
squeaked — made a small, high sound
trapped — caught and unable to move
roared — made a loud, deep sound
grateful — thankful
Let's Think
  1. Why did the lion let the mouse go?
  2. How did the mouse help the lion later?
  3. Why is it good to be kind to others, even small ones?
Trace the Moral
A small friend can be a big help.
Now You Write
Write about a time a friend helped you.

The Fox and the Grapes

One hot day, Fox saw juicy purple grapes hanging high on a vine. 'Those grapes look delicious,' he said. He jumped and jumped, but the grapes were too high to reach. Fox tried again and again, but he could not jump high enough. At last, tired and hungry, Fox gave up. He walked away and said, 'Those grapes were probably sour anyway. I didn't want them.' But really, Fox was just disappointed that he could not reach them.

New Words
juicy — full of liquid, like a ripe fruit
delicious — tastes very good
disappointed — feeling sad because something did not happen
sour — a sharp, unpleasant taste
Let's Think
  1. What did Fox want to eat?
  2. Why couldn't Fox get the grapes?
  3. Why did Fox say the grapes were sour?
Trace the Moral
It is easy to blame what we cannot have.
Now You Write
Write about a time something was hard and how it felt.

The man grew hot and took off his coat all by himself. Being gentle and warm was stronger than being rough.

She picked up small stones, one by one, and dropped them into the pitcher. Plop, plop, plop! Slowly, the water rose higher and higher. At last, Crow could reach the water and drink. Her clever thinking saved the day.

idea — a thought or plan
Let's Think
  1. Why couldn't Crow drink the water at first?
  2. What clever idea did Crow have?
  3. What happened to the water as she dropped stones in?
Trace the Moral

The Bundle of Sticks

An old man had four sons who often argued. One day he gave each son a single stick and asked them to break it. Snap! Each stick broke easily. Then he tied many sticks into one bundle. 'Now try to break this,' he said. The sons tried and tried, but the bundle would not break. 'Alone, you are weak like one stick,' said the father. 'Together, you are strong like this bundle.' The brothers promised to help each other from then on.

together — with others
Let's Think

Belling the Cat

A sneaky cat kept catching mice in the house. The mice held a meeting to solve the problem. 'Let's tie a bell around the cat's neck,' said a young mouse. 'Then we will hear her coming and run away!' All the mice cheered, what a wonderful plan! But then an old, wise mouse asked, 'That is a fine idea. But who will go and tie the bell on the cat?' The room went silent. No mouse was brave enough to try.

brave — not afraid
Let's Think
  1. What plan did the young mouse suggest?
  2. What question did the old mouse ask?

The Oak and the Reeds

A tall, strong Oak tree grew next to some thin, bending Reeds by a river. 'How do you stay standing in the wind?' asked Oak. 'You are so thin and weak!' One night, a huge storm came. The wind blew hard. The stiff Oak would not bend at all, crack, it broke and fell down. The Reeds, though thin, bent low with the wind and did not break. When the storm ended, the Reeds stood back up tall again.

New Words
reeds — thin plants that grow near water
storm — strong wind and rain
stiff — not able to bend
flexible — able to bend easily
Let's Think
  1. Who broke in the storm, the Oak or the Reeds?
  2. Why did the Reeds survive the storm?
  3. What does this story teach about being flexible?
Trace the Moral
Bending is sometimes stronger than breaking.
Now You Write
Write about a time you had to be flexible.

Fox invited Stork to dinner and served soup in a flat, shallow dish. Fox could lick it up easily, but Stork's long thin beak could not scoop up a single drop! Stork went home hungry. The Farmer and the Stork

Some cranes were eating seeds from a farmer's field, so he set a trap. He caught the cranes, and one stork who was standing with them, though she had eaten nothing. 'Please let me go,' said Stork. 'I am not a crane, and I ate none of your seeds. I was only visiting my friends.' 'That may be true,' said the farmer, 'but I found you with the thieves, so you will share their punishment.'

New Words
trap — a device used to catch an animal
punishment — a consequence for doing something wrong
company — the people or animals you spend time with
affect — to change or influence
Let's Think
  1. Why did the farmer set a trap in his field?
  2. Why was the stork caught too?
  3. What does this story teach about choosing friends?
