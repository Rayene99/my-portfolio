Standards Reference

Accessibility Guidelines

A practical reference based on WCAG 2.1 and Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles.

Topics
WCAG 2.1 Overview

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 are the international standard for digital accessibility, published by the W3C. They apply to any digital content — websites, PDFs, videos, LMS pages, and course materials.

The Four Principles (POUR)

PerceivableInformation must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive — including text alternatives for non-text content, captions for video, and content that can be presented in multiple ways.
OperableUsers must be able to operate all functionality via keyboard alone, and content must not cause seizures or require precise timing.
UnderstandableText must be readable and predictable, and users must be helped to avoid and correct mistakes.
RobustContent must be interpreted reliably by a wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies like screen readers and braille displays.

Conformance Levels

WCAG defines three levels: Level A (minimum), Level AA (the institutional and legal standard), and Level AAA (highest). This audit targets Level AA conformance — the standard required by most accessibility laws and university policies.

Why It Matters

Students with disabilities include those who are blind or have low vision, are d/Deaf or hard of hearing, have motor disabilities, or have cognitive differences. Accessible materials also benefit all students: captions help in noisy environments, clear headings help everyone navigate long documents, and plain language benefits non-native English speakers.

Document Accessibility

Most course materials are shared as PDFs, Word documents, or slides. Each format has specific requirements that must be met before distribution.

Heading Structure

Use built-in heading styles (Heading 1, Heading 2, etc.) rather than manually bolding or enlarging text. Screen readers use heading structure to let users jump between sections without reading every word.

✓ Correct
Apply "Heading 2" from the Styles panel in Word or Google Docs for all section titles
✗ Avoid
Select text → Bold → Increase font size to 16pt. This looks like a heading but provides no structure.

Alternative Text

Every meaningful image needs descriptive alt text that conveys what the image communicates, not just what it depicts. Decorative images should be marked decorative so screen readers skip them.

✓ Good alt text
"Bar chart showing graduation rates by income quintile 2015–2023, with the top quintile consistently 20+ points above the bottom."
✗ Poor alt text
"Chart image" or "Figure 3" — provides no information about what the chart shows.

PDF Accessibility

PDFs must be "tagged" — a structured representation that screen readers can interpret. Tagged PDFs are created when you export from Word with the accessibility option enabled. Scanned PDFs are image files and are completely inaccessible until run through OCR software.

Quick test: If you can highlight text by clicking and dragging in your PDF viewer, it is text-based. If clicking selects nothing, it is a scanned image and is inaccessible to screen readers.

Multimedia Accessibility

All video, audio, and interactive content used in your course must be accessible to students who cannot hear the audio or see the visual content.

Closed Captions

Closed captions are a synchronized text representation of all spoken audio in a video, including speaker identification and relevant sound effects. Auto-generated captions from YouTube, Panopto, or Zoom are a starting point only — they typically reach 80–90% accuracy, which is insufficient. You must review and correct captions before sharing them with students.

Caption quality standardsCaptions must be verbatim (or close to it), synchronized within 2 seconds, identify speakers when multiple people are speaking, and include relevant non-speech information such as [laughter] or [music playing].

Transcripts

Audio-only content (podcasts, recorded lectures) requires a complete text transcript. Transcripts benefit d/Deaf students, those with auditory processing differences, students who prefer to read, and anyone searching for specific content in a long recording.

Audio Descriptions

When visual information is essential to understanding your video — a diagram appearing on screen, text being written on a whiteboard — an audio description must narrate what is happening visually. The simplest approach: narrate your own actions as you record. "I'm drawing a flowchart now, starting with the input node at the top..."

Color & Contrast

Approximately 8% of men and 0.5% of women have some form of color vision deficiency. Many more have low vision or view content in challenging lighting conditions. Sufficient contrast and redundant coding are essential.

Contrast Ratios — WCAG Level AA

Normal text (under 18pt, or 14pt bold)Minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 between text color and background color.
Large text (18pt+, or 14pt+ bold)Minimum contrast ratio of 3:1 against the background color.
UI components and graphicsMinimum contrast ratio of 3:1 against adjacent colors for icons, charts, and interface elements.

Free tool: WebAIM's Contrast Checker at webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker/ — enter hex color codes to get an instant pass/fail result.

Color Is Not the Only Means

WCAG 1.4.1 requires that color not be used as the only visual means of conveying information. This applies to charts, graphs, diagrams, and any color-coded content in your materials.

✓ Accessible
In a line chart, use distinct colors AND different line patterns (solid, dashed, dotted) AND a text legend with labels for each series.
✗ Inaccessible
Using only color to distinguish chart lines, or marking correct/incorrect answers with only green/red text.
Plain Language Guide

Plain language is writing that is clear, direct, and easy for your audience to understand the first time they read it. It benefits all students, especially those with cognitive disabilities, learning differences, and students for whom English is a second language.

Core Principles

Use active voiceActive voice is clearer and more direct. Write "Submit your assignment by Friday" not "Assignments should be submitted by Friday."
Keep sentences shortAim for sentences under 25 words. One idea per sentence. Use bullet points for lists of three or more items rather than long compound sentences.
Define jargon and acronymsSpell out all acronyms on first use: "Learning Management System (LMS)." Define technical terms when they first appear, especially for introductory courses.
Use everyday words"Use" instead of "utilize." "Start" instead of "initiate." "Show" instead of "demonstrate." Unless the technical term is itself a learning objective for your course.

Readability Tools

Microsoft Word has a built-in Flesch-Kincaid readability score (enable it in File → Options → Proofing → Show readability statistics). The Hemingway Editor at hemingwayapp.com highlights complex sentences, passive voice, and hard-to-read passages in real time — for free.

✓ Plain
"Submit your essay as a PDF by 11:59 PM on Friday. Late work loses 10% per day."
✗ Dense
"All written assignments are to be submitted in PDF format no later than 11:59 PM on the designated due date, with subsequent late submission penalties of 10% per diem."
Universal Design for Learning

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) is a framework developed by CAST that guides the design of flexible learning experiences. Rather than retrofitting accommodations for individual students after the fact, UDL proactively builds flexibility into course design from the start.

The Three UDL Principles

Multiple Means of RepresentationPresent information in more than one format. Pair lecture with slides and a reading. Use examples, diagrams, and real-world illustrations alongside abstract concepts. Provide both charts and underlying data tables.
Multiple Means of Action & ExpressionAllow students to demonstrate what they know in different ways. Offer alternatives to traditional written exams — presentations, portfolios, creative projects, or demonstrations — when your learning objectives permit.
Multiple Means of EngagementOffer choices in how students engage with material. Foster collaboration alongside independent work. Develop self-assessment and reflection. Minimize unnecessary barriers to participation — not as lowered expectations, but as flexible pathways to the same rigorous outcomes.

Practical Applications

Representation: Post lecture slides before class. Offer written and video instructions for assignments. Include both graphs and data tables. Pre-teach key vocabulary.

Expression: Allow students to choose between a written essay, a recorded presentation, or an annotated portfolio to demonstrate the same learning outcome.

Engagement: Vary assessment types across a semester. Allow some choice in discussion topics. Give students the "why" behind course requirements.

UDL does not mean eliminating standards. It means intentional design that anticipates learner variability and builds in options, so fewer students require individual accommodations.
