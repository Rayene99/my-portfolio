Quick start

Build your practice routine

Twenty minutes a day, every day, beats three hours once a week. Your brain needs frequent, short contact with English to move words and patterns into long-term memory — this is why a routine matters more than any single method.

Pick your daily dose All levels

LevelMinutes / daySuggested split
Beginner15–20 minVocabulary review (40%) · Listening (40%) · Speaking aloud (20%)
Intermediate30–40 minListening/speaking (40%) · Reading (30%) · Vocabulary & grammar (30%)
Advanced45–60+ minReal-world input — podcasts, articles, conversation (60%) · Targeted gaps (40%)

A sample 20-minute session Beginner

0–5 min · Review

Flip through yesterday's flashcards or word journal. Say each word out loud before checking the meaning.

5–15 min · Input

Listen to one short clip (a beginner podcast, a simple video) twice — once for the general idea, once while reading the transcript.

15–20 min · Output

Say three sentences out loud about your day, using one new word from today.

Make it stick All levels

  • Anchor it to a habit you already have — practice while your coffee brews, during your commute, or right after you brush your teeth.
  • Lower the bar on hard days. A two-minute session that keeps the streak alive is worth more than a missed day "saved" for later.
  • Batch the long stuff for weekends — extensive reading, a full movie without subtitles, a longer conversation exchange.
  • Track it visibly. Use the weekly planner further down this guide to log your sessions.
Field assignment

Right now: open your calendar or phone reminders and block out one fixed time slot for tomorrow. Name it "English — 20 min." A scheduled habit survives; a vague intention rarely does.

Specimen collection

Vocabulary

Collecting words is like collecting specimens on a field trip: a word you only glance at gets forgotten by tomorrow, but one you handle, label, and use survives. This section gives you the methods first, then leveled word banks to start collecting from today.

Remember words for good All levels

Spaced repetition

Review a new word on a growing schedule: day 1, day 2, day 4, day 7, day 14, day 30. Each review pushes it further into long-term memory. This is the single most efficient way to stop forgetting — it's why flashcard apps exist at all.

Keep a word journal

For every new word, write: the word itself, a definition in your own words, an example sentence about your own life (not the dictionary's), and one "word friend" — a word it's commonly paired with.

Learn chunks, not single words

Native speakers store language in ready-made blocks. Learn make a decision, not "make" and "decision" separately — that's why "do a mistake" sounds wrong even though both words are correct on their own.

Grammar

You don't need a grammar textbook in your backpack — you need a map you can glance at when you forget which tense fits, and a short list of the mistakes that give learners away most often.

The tense map All levels

LevelTense / formUse it forExample
Beg.Present simpleFacts & habits"She works in a bank."
Beg.Present continuousHappening right now"I'm writing an email right now."
Beg.Past simpleFinished actions"We watched a movie last night."
Int.Present perfectExperience / unfinished time"I have visited Japan twice."
Int.Past continuousBackground action interrupted"I was cooking when she called."
Int.Will / going toFuture plans & predictions"I'll call you tomorrow."
Int.1st & 2nd conditionalReal & hypothetical situations"If I had more time, I would travel more."
Adv.Present perfect continuousOngoing action with duration"I've been studying since six."
Adv.Past perfectThe "earlier past""The show had already started when we arrived."
Adv.3rd & mixed conditionalUnreal past situations"If I had known, I would have come earlier."
Adv.Passive voiceThe action matters more than who did it"The bridge was built in 1932."
Adv.Reported speechRetelling what someone said"She said she was tired."

Mistakes that give learners away All levels

Articles Beg.

I am engineer.
I am an engineer. — singular countable nouns almost always need a/an/the.

Subject–verb agreement Beg.

She don't like coffee.
She doesn't like coffee. — he/she/it takes "does/doesn't" and an -s on the verb.

Prepositions of time Int.

See you in Monday.
See you on Monday. — use on for days/dates, in for months/years, at for clock times.

Countable vs. uncountable Int.

I have many informations.
I have a lot of information. — information, advice, news, and furniture have no plural.

Adjective order Int.

a red big house
a big red house — size usually comes before color.

"Bored" vs. "boring" Adv.

I am boring in this meeting.
I am bored in this meeting. — -ed describes how you feel; -ing describes what causes the feeling.

Out in the open

Speaking & listening

This is the pair most learners avoid practicing alone, because it feels like it needs another person. It doesn't. Every method below works solo, with nothing but your own voice and your phone.

Talk to yourself, on purpose All levels

Narrate what you're doing as you do it: "I'm making coffee. Now I'm putting the cup down." Describe your day on your commute, out loud or under your breath. This feels strange for about three days and then becomes completely normal — and it's the fastest way to notice exactly which words you're missing.

Shadowing: borrow a native speaker's mouth Intermediate

Shadowing means speaking at the same time as a recording, copying its rhythm and melody, not just its words.

  1. Pick 30–60 seconds of audio at or slightly above your level, with a transcript.
  2. Listen once for the general meaning.
  3. Listen again while reading along.
  4. Play it a third time and speak with it, a half-second behind, matching the speaker's stress and intonation rather than your own.
  5. Repeat the same clip 3–5 times. It should feel easier and more natural each time.

Try a shadowing clip now

"I can't believe how quickly this year has gone by. It feels like just yesterday we were planning the summer trip, and now we're already talking about the holidays."

Record yourself and listen back All levels

Use your phone's voice memo app to record yourself talking for one minute about anything. Listen back the next day, not immediately — with fresh ears you'll hear your own filler words, pacing, and pronunciation far more clearly. Keep a running note of one thing to fix each time, not ten.

Find a conversation partner Intermediate

A language exchange works best with light structure: agree to split the time 50/50 between both languages, and bring two or three topics so you're never stuck choosing one on the spot. Here are starter prompts by level — press 🔊 to hear each one before your next call.

Conversation starters Beginner
  • "What did you do this weekend?"
  • "What's your favorite food, and why?"
  • "Can you describe your morning routine?"
Conversation starters Intermediate
  • "What's a skill you'd like to learn, and why?"
  • "Would you rather live in a big city or the countryside?"
  • "What's a piece of advice you'd give your younger self?"
Conversation starters Advanced
  • "Should governments regulate social media more closely?"
  • "Is it better to specialize deeply or be broadly competent?"
  • "How has technology changed the way you think — for better or worse?"

Build a listening habit by level All levels

Beginner

Short clips (1–3 min) made for learners, slow and clearly enunciated. Turn on English subtitles — never your native language — and replay the same clip until it feels easy.

Intermediate

Real podcasts and shows on topics you already enjoy. Keep English subtitles on for shows, drop them for podcasts once you can follow the gist.

Advanced

Native-speed news, interviews, and audiobooks with no subtitles at all. Let yourself miss occasional words — total comprehension isn't the goal, fluent tracking of meaning is.

Field assignment

Today: record sixty seconds of yourself describing your day. Don't listen back until tomorrow — then note one specific thing to improve, and try the same sixty seconds again.

Sound notes

Pronunciation

Clear pronunciation isn't about sounding "native" — it's about being effortless to understand. A handful of sounds and stress patterns cause most of the friction. This section uses American English throughout, with a 🔊 button on almost every example so you can hear it before you try it.

Word stress can change meaning Intermediate

Many two-syllable English words switch meaning depending on which syllable is stressed — usually noun (stress on the first syllable) versus verb (stress on the second).

WordAs a nounAs a verb
present PRE-sent — a gift pre-SENT — to give/show
record RE-cord — an achievement re-CORD — to capture audio
object OB-ject — a thing ob-JECT — to disagree

Sentence stress & rhythm Advanced

English doesn't give every word equal weight. Content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives) carry the stress; function words (articles, prepositions, pronouns) get compressed and quiet. That contrast is what gives English its rhythm.

Listen for the heavy beats

"I think we should leave before it gets dark."

Yes/no questions usually rise at the end ("Are you coming? ↗"); statements and wh-questions usually fall ("Where are you going? ↘").

Connected speech: how English actually sounds Advanced

In casual speech, native speakers blend words together. Recognizing these is essential for listening, even though you wouldn't write them this way in anything formal.

Full formCasual soundExample
going to"gonna" "I'm gonna call you later."
want to"wanna" "Do you wanna grab lunch?"
have to"hafta" "I hafta go now."
did you"didja" "Didja see that?"

On the page

Reading & writing

Reading and writing won't fix your accent, but they quietly build the vocabulary and sentence patterns that speaking depends on. A little, often, is enough.

Read for volume, not perfection All levels

Choose material where you already understand roughly 95–98% of the words. Keep reading through unfamiliar words instead of stopping for the dictionary every time — guess from context first, and only look up a word if it repeats and still blocks meaning. This is called extensive reading, and it works because volume builds intuition that rule-memorizing can't.

Beginner

Graded readers written for your level, or simplified news sites that control vocabulary and sentence length on purpose.

Intermediate

Young-adult fiction, blog posts, or news articles on subjects you already know something about.

Advanced

Unsimplified novels, long-form journalism, opinion essays — anything you'd genuinely choose to read in your own language.

Daily journal prompts All levels

Five sentences a day, in a notebook or a notes app, builds more writing fluency than one long essay a month.

  • Beg. "What did you eat today, and what did you like or dislike about it?"
  • Int. "Describe a small decision you made today and why you chose it."
  • Adv. "Argue for or against something you usually agree with — try the opposite side."

A two-minute self-editing checklist Intermediate

Before you consider anything "finished," scan it once for each of these:

  • Does every sentence have a subject and a verb that agree (he goes, not he go)?
  • Did you stay in one tense unless you have a clear reason to switch?
  • Did you check a/an/the in front of every singular countable noun?
  • Are any sentences so long you lost track of your own point? Split them.
Field assignment

Today: write five sentences answering the journal prompt at your level. Tomorrow, reread them with the self-editing checklist before writing five new ones.

Idioms & phrasal verbs

This is the vocabulary that makes you sound like you actually live in the language, not just study it. Learn these the same way as the word banks above — as whole chunks, with their own example sentence.

Everyday phrasal verbs Beginner
  • look forto search for something."I'm looking for my keys."
  • give upto stop trying."Don't give up on your goals."
  • figure outto understand or solve something."I finally figured out the problem."
  • put offto postpone."Don't put off your homework until the last minute."
  • run intoto meet someone by chance."I ran into an old friend at the store."
Talking about time Intermediate
  • in the nick of timejust before it's too late."We arrived in the nick of time."
  • once in a blue moonvery rarely."He visits his hometown once in a blue moon."
  • around the clockall day and night, without stopping."The team worked around the clock to fix the bug."
Work & productivity Intermediate
  • get the ball rollingto start something."Let's get the ball rolling on the new project."
  • cut cornersto do something poorly or cheaply to save time/money."Don't cut corners on safety checks."
  • on the same pagein agreement, understanding things the same way."Before we start, let's make sure we're on the same page."
Emotions & social situations Advanced
  • bite the bulletto face something unpleasant with courage."I bit the bullet and asked for a raise."
  • break the iceto ease tension in a social situation."He told a joke to break the ice."
  • read between the linesto understand a hidden or implied meaning."If you read between the lines, she's not happy with the decision."
  • on thin icein a risky position, close to trouble."He's on thin ice after missing another deadline."

Last page

Mindset

Progress in a language is rarely a straight line — plateaus are normal, not a sign you're doing it wrong. A few mental habits make the difference between learners who keep going and learners who quit at the plateau.

  • Practice just above your level. Input that's a little harder than what you already know teaches you something; input that's far too hard just frustrates you.
  • Consistency beats intensity. Fifteen minutes, five days a week, will take you further than one exhausting Sunday session.
  • Treat mistakes as data, not failure. Notice it, fix it once, move on. Dwelling on an error wastes more time than the error itself.
  • Use English for things you already love. Gaming, cooking videos, music, sports commentary — motivation follows genuine interest far better than any worksheet.
  • Track small wins. That's exactly what the planner and tracker in this guide are for — progress is easier to see in checkboxes than in memory.

Every conversation, every clumsy sentence, every replayed clip is a specimen collected. Keep collecting.
