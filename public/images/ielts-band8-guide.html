Step 01 — Registration

How to Sit for the
Computer-Delivered IELTS

The computer-delivered IELTS (CD-IELTS) is offered by both IDP and British Council. You complete Listening, Reading, and Writing on a computer; Speaking remains a face-to-face interview with an examiner (or video call). Here is the complete process:

💡 Why choose Computer-Delivered? Faster results (3–5 days vs 13 days for paper), more test dates available per month, easier to edit writing (cut/paste/delete), and on-screen countdown timers. Most test-takers score the same or slightly better on CD.
1

Choose Your Test Version

Decide between IELTS Academic (for university admissions, professional registration) or IELTS General Training (for migration, secondary school). Most universities and skilled migration programs require Academic. Confirm with your institution before booking.

2

Select a Test Centre & Date

  • Go to ielts.idp.com (IDP) or ielts.britishcouncil.org (British Council)
  • Enter your country and city to see available centres
  • CD-IELTS is offered up to 7 days a week at many centres — far more frequently than paper tests
  • Book at least 4–6 weeks ahead of your desired date to secure a slot
3

Create an Account & Register

  • Create an account on the chosen provider's website
  • Enter personal details exactly as they appear on your passport or national ID
  • Upload a clear photo of your ID document
  • Select your test date, time slot, and centre
4

Pay the Test Fee

The fee varies by country — typically USD $185–$260 / GBP £195–£220. Payment is usually by credit/debit card online. You will receive a confirmation email with your booking reference.

5

Prepare Your ID & Arrive on Test Day

  • Bring the same valid ID used during registration (passport strongly recommended)
  • Arrive 30 minutes before your test start time
  • You will store all personal belongings in a locker — no phones, no notes
  • You will be assigned a computer workstation with headphones (for Listening)
  • A short tutorial on how to use the computer interface is shown before the test begins
6

Speaking Test Scheduling

Speaking is scheduled separately — either on the same day as the other tests or within 7 days before or after. You will be told your Speaking slot when you book. It is a 11–14 minute face-to-face interview with a certified examiner.

7

Receive Your Results

CD-IELTS results are available online in 3–5 calendar days after your test date. You will receive one Test Report Form (TRF) with your overall band and four section band scores. Additional TRF copies can be sent directly to institutions.

📋 Computer Interface Tips (on test day) Use the on-screen highlighter and flag tool during Reading to mark tricky questions. In Writing, the word count updates in real time — no manual counting needed. You can navigate freely between questions within a section before time is up.

Step 02 — Understanding the Target

What Band 8 Actually Means

IELTS scores are reported on a 0–9 band scale. Each section (Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking) receives a band score. These are averaged to produce the Overall Band Score, rounded to the nearest 0.5.

Band Level Description % of Test-Takers
9 Expert Full operational command, fluent and precise ~1%
8.5 Very Good Occasional unsystematic errors; handles complex argument well ~2%
8 Fully operational with only occasional inaccuracies ~3%
7.5 Good Generally effective command; some inaccuracies in unfamiliar contexts ~6%
7 Handles complex language; occasional errors and misunderstandings ~11%
6 Competent Effective command despite inaccuracies; understands complex language ~25%
🎯 What scores do you need per section for Band 8 overall? To average Band 8, you need roughly Listening 8.5 + Reading 8.5 + Writing 7.5 + Speaking 7.5, OR more balanced scores like 8 + 8 + 8 + 8. Writing and Speaking are the hardest sections to push to 8+, so over-perform in Listening and Reading to give yourself a buffer.

Step 03 — Section Strategies

Band 8+ Strategy for
Every Section

Listening · 30 min

Listening

  • Read questions BEFORE the audio plays — predict the answer type (number, name, date, noun)
  • Listen for paraphrase: the audio rarely uses the exact words in the question
  • Sections 3 & 4 (academic discussions) are hardest — focus extra energy there
  • Never leave a blank — guess if uncertain; wrong answers do not penalise extra
  • Check spelling carefully; misspelled correct answers are marked wrong
  • Practice daily with BBC Radio 4, TED Talks, and IELTS practice tests at 1.0–1.1× speed
Reading · 60 min

Reading

  • Aim to spend ~17–20 min per passage; 3 passages total = 40 questions
  • Skim the passage first (45 sec), then read questions, then scan for answers
  • True/False/Not Given: "Not Given" means the information is simply absent — do not infer
  • Matching Headings: identify the core idea of each paragraph first
  • Build vocabulary by reading The Economist, Nature, and academic journals weekly
  • Never spend more than 90 seconds on one question — flag and move on
Writing · 60 min

Writing

  • Task 1 (20 min): Describe, compare, and highlight key trends — never just list data. Include an overview paragraph summarising the main trend
  • Task 2 (40 min): Spend 5 minutes planning before writing; a structured essay beats an unplanned one every time
  • Use a wide range of grammatical structures: conditionals, passives, relative clauses, participle clauses
  • Vary sentence length: short for emphasis, long for elaboration
  • Task 2: 4 clear paragraphs — Intro → Body 1 → Body 2 → Conclusion (250–280 words ideal)
  • Get your writing marked by a certified IELTS teacher at least 4× during prep
Speaking · 11–14 min

Speaking

  • Part 1 (4–5 min): Answer naturally, not too short, not rehearsed-sounding. Extend answers with a reason or example
  • Part 2 (3–4 min): Use all 1 minute of preparation time; note 3–4 bullet points; speak for the full 2 minutes
  • Part 3 (4–5 min): Give opinions with justifications; use discourse markers ("On the one hand…", "It could be argued that…")
  • Pronunciation > accent: be clear and varied in pitch; do not try to fake a foreign accent
  • Record yourself weekly and listen back critically
  • Use idiomatic phrases naturally — forced idioms hurt your score
📊 IELTS Writing Band Descriptors (what examiners look for) Task Achievement/Response · Coherence & Cohesion · Lexical Resource · Grammatical Range & Accuracy — all four carry equal weight (25% each). Most candidates lose marks on Lexical Resource and Grammatical Range. Prioritise variety, not just accuracy.

Step 04 — Language Building

Vocabulary & Grammar
for Band 8

At Band 8, examiners expect you to use language that is precise, varied, and natural. Below are the language-building priorities that separate a Band 7 from a Band 8.

V

Lexical Resource: Move Beyond "Good" Words

  • Replace overused words: important → pivotal / crucial / integral, increase → surge / escalate / climb, problem → predicament / impediment / challenge
  • Learn collocations, not just individual words: reach a consensus, pose a threat, wield influence
  • Practise topic-specific vocabulary: environment, technology, health, education, urbanisation — these appear most in IELTS
  • Target 20 new high-frequency academic words per week using Anki or a vocabulary notebook
G

Grammatical Range: Structures That Impress

  • Inversion for emphasis: "Not only does this policy reduce costs, but it also…"
  • Cleft sentences: "It is the lack of regulation that poses the greatest risk."
  • Mixed conditionals: "If stricter laws had been passed, the situation would be different today."
  • Participial clauses: "Having examined the data, researchers concluded that…"
  • Passive voice for academic objectivity: use it strategically, not randomly
C

Cohesion: Make Your Writing Flow

  • Use a variety of cohesive devices — not just "furthermore" and "however" every sentence
  • Employ reference words: this, these, such, the former, the latter
  • Vary sentence openers: start with a subordinate clause, a gerund, an adverb, or an inversion
  • Avoid bullet points or lists in Writing Task 2 — write in full, connected paragraphs

Step 05 — Study Plan

Your 12-Week Band 8
Preparation Plan

This plan assumes 1.5–2 hours of focused daily study. Adjust timelines based on your current level — if you are already at Band 6.5+, 8 weeks may suffice.

Period Phase Focus & Activities
Weeks 1–2 Diagnostic Take 1 full timed practice test. Identify your weakest sections. Study band descriptors for Writing & Speaking. Set up vocabulary notebook and Anki deck.
Weeks 3–4 Foundation Grammar deep-dive (conditionals, passives, complex sentences). 20 new vocabulary items/week. Read 1 academic article daily (highlight collocations). Practice Listening Sections 1 & 2 daily.
Weeks 5–6 Skills Build Write 3× Task 1 essays + 3× Task 2 essays per week. Focus on Reading question types (T/F/NG, Matching). Practice Speaking Parts 1 & 2 daily (record yourself). Listening Sections 3 & 4.
Weeks 7–8 Skills Build Get Writing marked by a qualified IELTS tutor (aim for 2 essays reviewed). Continue 20 vocab/week. Mock Speaking session with a partner or tutor. Full Listening test twice per week.
Weeks 9–10 Intensive 2 full timed mock tests (all 4 sections). Analyse every error — categorise by question type. Targeted drilling of weak areas only. Reading speed drills: aim for 250+ words/minute with comprehension.
Weeks 11–12 Peak & Polish 1 full mock test per week under exam conditions (same time of day as your booked test). Light review of vocabulary and grammar notes. Focus on exam technique, timing, and managing nerves. No new material in final 4 days.
📅 Daily Schedule Template (1.5 hours) 15 min — Vocabulary review (Anki / notebook)  |  30 min — Reading practice or Writing draft  |  20 min — Listening exercise  |  20 min — Speaking (record + review OR grammar study)  |  5 min — Review errors from yesterday

Step 06 — Test Day

Exam Day Checklist
& Mindset

DAY
-1

The Night Before

  • Do NOT study or do practice tests — your brain needs consolidation rest
  • Prepare your ID, confirmation email, and route to the test centre
  • Sleep 7–8 hours; avoid alcohol and heavy food
  • Light review of your own grammar/vocabulary notes only (max 30 min)
DAY

On the Day

  • Eat a proper breakfast — blood sugar affects concentration significantly
  • Arrive 30 minutes early; bring your passport/ID and confirmation
  • Use the on-screen tutorial time to familiarise yourself with navigation buttons
  • In Listening: if you miss an answer, let it go and focus on the next one
  • In Reading: watch the time — do not spend more than 90 sec on any single question
  • In Writing: plan before you write. 5 minutes of planning saves 15 minutes of rewriting
  • In Speaking: speak at a natural pace. Silence while thinking is fine — use fillers naturally ("That's an interesting point…")
